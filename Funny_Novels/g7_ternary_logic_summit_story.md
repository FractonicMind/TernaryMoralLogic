# The Great G7 Binder Fiasco: A Comedy of Ternary Errors

*Being the Completely True and Undoubtedly Accurate Account of How Seven World Leaders and One Confused Economic Strategist Accidentally Stumbled Upon the Future of Global Governance*

---

As the Chief Economic Strategist for Global Stability—a title that sounds more impressive than it actually is, kind of like being "Supreme Commander of the Napkin Folding Division"—I had always prided myself on being prepared for any diplomatic crisis. Bank runs? I'd seen them. Currency collapses? Tuesday for me. International trade disputes? I had a entire filing cabinet dedicated to those.

But nothing, and I mean absolutely nothing, had prepared me for what happened at the emergency G7 summit in Geneva that fateful Tuesday in December.

The setting was the Grand Ballroom of the Hotel des Bergues, because apparently world leaders have the same taste in venues as over-enthused wedding planners. Eight leather-upholstered chairs arranged in a perfect circle, each one representing not just a country, but an entire civilization's hopes, dreams, and various economic policies that probably made sense to someone, somewhere.

I had spent weeks preparing for this moment. The "World Stability Outlook 2030" report was my magnum opus—a 847-page document that analyzed every conceivable global risk with the thoroughness of a tax auditor and the optimism of a weather reporter. It was the kind of comprehensive analysis that only someone who had never actually met a world leader could write.

President Harrison Blackwood of the United States was the first to arrive, which was fitting since Americans have this peculiar ability to be both early and fashionably late at the same time. Tall, distinguished, with the kind of silver hair that screams "I could either be a very successful lawyer or a very successful criminal," he shook my hand with the kind of grip that suggested he was either trying to prove his masculinity or check my pulse.

"Strategist!" he boomed, because apparently we were on a first-name basis now. "Ready to save the world? I've got my thinking cap on."

I nodded politely, wondering where one acquired such a cap. Did it come with the presidency, like a kit?

Prime Minister Victoria Pemberton of the United Kingdom arrived next, looking like she'd stepped out of a Jane Austen novel if Jane Austen had written about international finance. Everything about her screamed "proper" and "constitutional," from her perfectly tailored blazer to the way she held her teacup like she was performing surgery.

"Good morning, everyone," she said with that particular British inflection that makes everything sound both intelligent and mildly disappointed. "I do hope we're not going to be discussing anything too... American today."

Chancellor Helmut Weissmann of Germany entered with the precision of a Swiss watchmaker and the intensity of someone who had just discovered his neighbor's Wi-Fi password. Everything about him suggested efficiency, except for his hair, which seemed to have its own agenda entirely.

"The economic data from last quarter shows concerning trends in the manufacturing sector," he announced before anyone could say hello. "We must address this immediately."

"Oh, for crying out loud," muttered Prime Minister Giuseppe Torrino of Italy, who looked like he'd just stepped off a Mediterranean yacht and into a boardroom by mistake. "Can't we at least have coffee first? What is this, Germany?"

President Marie-Claire Dubois of France swept in like she owned the room, which, given France's approach to international relations, she probably thought she did. Her accent was so pure it could have been exported, and her outfit screamed "I am intellectually superior to all of you" in four different languages.

"Bonjour, mes amis," she announced. "I trust we are here to discuss something worthy of French intellectual input?"

Prime Minister Akiko Tanaka of Japan entered with the kind of respectful bow that made everyone else suddenly very aware of their posture. She carried herself with the quiet dignity of someone who had spent considerable time perfecting the art of looking interested while internally calculating tax rates.

"Konnichiwa," she said simply. "I am prepared to contribute constructively to our discussions."

Prime Minister Jean-Philippe Bouchard of Canada looked like the kind of person who would apologize if someone else stepped on his toe. Friendly, approachable, and carrying himself with that particular Canadian mix of politeness and mild concern about disappointing people.

"So," he said cheerfully, "what are we talking about today? Infrastructure? Climate? That thing with the whales again?"

And finally, President Elena Volkov of the European Commission arrived with the kind of dramatic flair that suggested she'd either been an opera singer in a previous life or had consumed far too much espresso. Her presence filled the room with the energy of someone who genuinely believed bureaucracy could solve anything.

"Gentlemen, ladies," she announced, "let us begin. I have prepared seventeen different position papers on today's agenda."

I cleared my throat—the kind of throat clearing that suggested I was about to do something important that would probably go terribly wrong.

"Distinguished leaders," I began, "today we will be reviewing the World Stability Outlook 2030, a comprehensive analysis of global economic risks and mitigation strategies. I've prepared detailed briefings on each section."

The room fell silent in that particular way that suggests people are either very interested or very concerned about what comes next.

President Blackwood leaned forward. "What's our biggest threat, Strategist?"

"Well, sir, according to the data, we face significant challenges from systemic risks in high-frequency trading, regulatory capture issues, and the general opacity of algorithmic decision-making in financial markets—"

"Oh, for heaven's sake," interrupted Chancellor Weissmann, "just give us the summary."

"Of course. Essentially, we're all flying blind through increasingly complex economic systems with no real way to verify what's actually happening, and when things go wrong, we have to piece together what happened from incomplete, potentially corrupted information. It's like trying to solve a murder mystery where half the evidence has been deliberately destroyed."

Prime Minister Pemberton raised an eyebrow. "That sounds... concerning. What do you propose?"

"Well, that's where today's discussion was supposed to focus. The report outlines several potential solutions involving enhanced oversight, improved reporting mechanisms, and—"

My assistant, poor panicked Marcus, chose that moment to burst into the room like he'd been chased by wolves.

"Sir! Sir, I have a problem!" he gasped, clutching a stack of binders to his chest like they were the Holy Grail. "I was supposed to prepare the briefing materials for each delegation, but I got confused during the color-coding process, and now I'm not entirely certain I have the right documents!"

I stared at him. "Marcus, please tell me you haven't accidentally given the President of France a copy of our agricultural subsidy analysis."

"No, sir! It's much worse than that!" He held up one of the binders, which bore the imposing title: "THE ARCHITECTURE OF ASSURED GOVERNANCE: Ternary Logic as a Sovereign, Evidentiary Triadic Framework for Global Economic Systems — Technical Standard & Research Monograph."

The room went dead silent. Even the chandelier seemed to stop swaying.

"Marcus," I said slowly, "please tell me that's not what I think it is."

"I'm afraid it is, sir. I was so worried about getting the formatting right that I may have... substituted the main briefing document with something from your research archive."

President Blackwood squinted at the binder. "Ternary Logic? Is that some kind of new math?"

"Well," I said, my mind racing, "it's more of a... comprehensive restructuring of economic governance based on triadic decision-making frameworks and evidentiary architectural requirements—"

"What does that even mean?" demanded President Dubois, looking like she'd just been asked to review a cookbook written in Ancient Sumerian.

Chancellor Weissmann was already flipping through the document. "This appears to be some sort of... technical specification? Strategist, is this a computer manual?"

"It's more of a... theoretical framework, actually. Very theoretical. Extremely theoretical. So theoretical that—"

"Will someone please just explain what this 'Ternary Logic' actually does?" interrupted Prime Minister Torrino. "Because from where I'm sitting, it sounds like someone took a really complicated way to say 'we need better record-keeping.'"

I opened my mouth to explain, but President Volkov had grabbed another copy and was reading aloud: "Pillar One: Epistemic Hold. The system must architecturally compel a pause when data is ambiguous or truth is uncertain, creating an immutable record that the institution exercised fiduciary duty and due care by pausing rather than acting recklessly."

Everyone stared at her.

"I'm sorry," said Prime Minister Pemberton, "what exactly did you just say?"

President Volkov looked up from the document, her eyes gleaming with the kind of excitement usually reserved for discovering a new flavor of gelato. "It appears to be describing a computational pause button! The system literally cannot proceed if it doesn't know what's happening!"

"That's..." President Blackwood paused. "That's actually not a terrible idea. How many economic disasters could we have avoided if someone had just told the computers to stop and think about it first?"

"But that would require them to admit they don't know something," observed Chancellor Weissmann dryly. "In my experience, most systems prefer to guess than to pause."

President Dubois was now holding a different section of the document. "Look at this! Pillar Two: Immutable Ledger. 'Every decision must generate a secured, immutable Decision Log proving regulatory compliance before the action is permitted. No Log = No Action.'"

Prime Minister Tanaka tilted her head. "So you're telling me that the system cannot operate without first documenting why it's operating?"

"Exactly!" I said, getting excited despite myself. "It's like requiring a shopping receipt before you can buy something, except it's for every economic decision ever made."

"That would be..." Prime Minister Pemberton looked thoughtful, "actually quite brilliant. Imagine being able to prove exactly why every financial decision was made, with cryptographic certainty."

President Blackwood was now reading over President Volkov's shoulder. "There's more! The Goukassian Principle: Lantern, Signature, License. Before any action, the system must verify its certainty, declare its intent, and receive computational permission to proceed."

"This is like a three-step process," mused Chancellor Weissmann. "First, check if you know what you're doing. Second, document what you think you're doing. Third, get permission to actually do it."

"Exactly!" I said. "It's the architectural realization of 'measure twice, cut once,' but for global finance!"

Prime Minister Torrino threw up his hands. "Wait, wait, wait. Let me get this straight. You're telling me that instead of trying to figure out what happened after everything goes wrong, this system would prevent anything from going wrong in the first place?"

"Well," I admitted, "it's more nuanced than that, but essentially yes."

"And it does this by... making the computers admit when they're confused?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"More than that," President Volkov continued reading. "It forces them to document their confusion, escalate it to humans, and wait for resolution. It's like a grown-up version of 'I don't know, let me ask.'"

President Blackwood sat back in his chair. "You know what's fascinating about this? It would eliminate most of our current regulatory problems by making them computationally impossible."

"Computationally impossible to do what?" asked Prime Minister Pemberton.

"To be non-compliant," said Chancellor Weissmann slowly, as if he was working through a complex equation. "If the system literally cannot execute a transaction without first proving regulatory compliance, then regulatory compliance becomes an architectural requirement, not a voluntary policy."

President Dubois was now holding a section that made her eyes widen. "Decision Logs! Schema-validated, pre-action documentation of every decision! Every single economic action must be recorded in a standardized format before it can happen!"

"This is incredible," said Prime Minister Tanaka quietly. "It's not just record-keeping; it's verifiable truth."

"Verifiable truth," repeated President Volkov. "Do you realize what this would mean for international trade disputes? We could settle arguments about what actually happened by checking the cryptographic evidence!"

Prime Minister Torrino was shaking his head. "This is too simple. If it was this easy, someone would have done it already."

"Ah," I said, "but that's the brilliant part. It's not easy at all. It's computationally intensive, architecturally complex, and requires a complete restructuring of how financial systems operate. The technical barriers are enormous."

"So how do we implement something like this?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

President Blackwood was now reading a section that made him frown. "Sustainability Capital Allocation Mandate. 'Economic systems must architecturally prevent activities that demonstrably harm long-term systemic stability.'"

"Sustainability," mused Chancellor Weissmann, "but not environmental sustainability—systemic sustainability. The system itself cannot be allowed to become unstable."

"That's brilliant," said President Dubois. "It's like building anti-fragility into the foundation of the system."

President Volkov looked up from the document. "There's something called the Hybrid Shield. It doesn't just detect fraud; it prevents it computationally."

"Prevents fraud?" asked Prime Minister Pemberton.

"The system blocks any transaction that exhibits manipulative behavior," I explained. "Instead of detecting fraud after the fact and trying to prove it, it simply doesn't allow fraudulent transactions to exist."

Prime Minister Tanaka was nodding slowly. "Like a computer that refuses to run a program if the program is obviously malicious."

"Exactly! And the final piece—Anchors. Every decision gets publicly anchored to blockchain networks, making the entire system's history publicly verifiable."

The room fell silent as the implications sank in.

"So let me understand this correctly," said President Blackwood slowly. "You're describing a financial system that cannot lie, cannot cheat, cannot make decisions without documenting why, and automatically prevents harmful actions?"

"That's... actually terrifying," said Prime Minister Pemberton.

"Why terrifying?" asked President Volkov.

"Because it would work," said Chancellor Weissmann. "If we implemented this globally, it would eliminate most of our current economic problems."

"But it would also eliminate most of our current economic advantages," objected Prime Minister Torrino. "Think of all the efficiency we'd lose! All the speed! The competitive edge!"

"What competitive edge?" demanded President Blackwood. "The edge of being able to hide what we're doing? The edge of regulatory arbitrage?"

President Dubois was reading another section. "Economic Rights and Transparency Mandate. Every individual has the right to verify the system's decisions about their economic activity."

"Citizens can check the blockchain to see exactly why they were denied a loan?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"That's what it says," confirmed President Volkov.

"Madness!" declared Prime Minister Torrino. "That would eliminate the entire industry of 'creative compliance'!"

"Good," said Chancellor Weissmann. "Creative compliance is just a fancy word for legal deception."

Prime Minister Tanaka looked thoughtful. "This would require complete transparency in algorithmic decision-making."

"Which means," said Prime Minister Pemberton slowly, "we'd have to actually make sure our algorithms were fair."

President Blackwood was pacing now. "Do you realize what this would do to market manipulation? If every decision had to be documented before it happened, and the documentation was cryptographically sealed..."

"High-frequency trading manipulation would become computationally impossible," finished President Volkov.

"And money laundering," added Chancellor Weissmann. "If every transaction had to prove its legitimacy before execution..."

"The AML compliance burden would disappear because non-compliant transactions couldn't exist," I said.

President Dubois threw up her hands. "It's too perfect! It's like someone designed a system specifically to make regulatory oversight obsolete!"

"Which brings us back to the question," said Prime Minister Torrino. "How do we implement something this comprehensive?"

The room fell quiet as everyone contemplated the magnitude of the task.

"Well," I said carefully, "the technical monograph outlines a phased implementation approach, starting with critical financial infrastructure and expanding outward..."

"No, no," interrupted President Blackwood. "The question isn't how to implement it. The question is: why aren't we already implementing it?"

Chancellor Weissmann frowned. "Because it's technically impossible to implement at the required scale?"

"Is it?" asked President Volkov. "Or do we just assume it's impossible because it would require unprecedented international cooperation?"

"International cooperation," mused Prime Minister Pemberton, "that rare commodity that even gold cannot buy."

Prime Minister Tanaka was reading something that made her frown. "The document mentions something called a 'No Switch Off' rule. Systems cannot be disabled to bypass the architectural constraints."

"Meaning?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"Meaning you couldn't turn off the transparency to get a competitive advantage," I explained.

"So it would be like speed limits for financial systems," said President Blackwood. "You can't disable them even if you want to go faster."

President Dubois was now reading with growing excitement. "Stewardship Custodians! Human oversight that cannot be corrupted! Distributed authority models that prevent regulatory capture!"

"Regulatory capture," repeated Chancellor Weissmann. "The ultimate enemy of financial stability."

"Which this system architecturally prevents," noted Prime Minister Pemberton. "Because the oversight cannot be controlled by the entities being overseen."

Prime Minister Torrino was shaking his head. "This is madness. You're describing a global financial system that is more transparent than anything that has ever existed in human history."

"And?" prompted President Volkov.

"And it might actually work," he admitted grudgingly.

President Blackwood looked around the room. "Let me understand what we're saying here. You're telling me that this random technical document that our panicked assistant accidentally brought instead of the briefing materials actually contains a comprehensive solution to every major economic problem we face?"

"It would appear so," I said.

"And it does this by... making it computationally impossible to be non-compliant?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"That's the general idea," I confirmed.

Prime Minister Tanaka was reading quietly. "The Epistemic Hold state. When the system doesn't know what to do, it pauses and escalates to human stewardship."

"Which is exactly what we do when we don't know what to do," observed President Volkov. "Why shouldn't our systems do the same?"

"Because humans are supposed to be smarter than computers," said Prime Minister Torrino.

"Are we?" asked Chancellor Weissmann dryly. "Because from where I'm sitting, the computers have been making a lot better decisions than the humans lately."

President Blackwood sat down heavily. "I need to think about this."

"There's more," said President Dubois, who had been reading ahead. "The document talks about triadic logic. Instead of binary decisions—yes or no—the system has three states: proceed, refuse, or pause."

"Pause when truth is uncertain," mused Prime Minister Pemberton. "Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is."

"It's the Goukassian Vow," I explained. "The foundational ethical mandate encoded into the system."

"Encoded into the system," repeated Chancellor Weissmann. "The system has ethics."

"The system has ethics," confirmed President Volkov. "Not policies about ethics. Actual, architectural ethics."

Prime Minister Tanaka looked up from the document. "And these ethics are enforced computationally?"

"Through every decision, every action, every transaction," I said.

President Blackwood ran his hands through his hair. "So you're telling me that instead of relying on human judgment to make ethical decisions in complex financial situations, we encode the ethics directly into the computational architecture?"

"That's exactly what I'm telling you."

"And that would prevent most financial crises?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"According to the analysis, it would prevent the kinds of financial crises that result from opacity, uncertainty, and the inability to verify what actually happened."

The room fell quiet again as the leaders processed this information.

"This would require complete international cooperation," said Prime Minister Torrino finally.

"Yes," I agreed.

"And it would mean sharing sensitive financial information across borders," added Prime Minister Pemberton.

"Yes," I agreed again.

"And it would make certain types of financial innovation impossible," noted Chancellor Weissmann.

"Yes," I agreed for the third time.

President Volkov looked around the room. "So what you're saying is that to implement this system, we would have to actually trust each other?"

"That's... an accurate summary," I said.

Prime Minister Tanaka smiled. "That might be the most revolutionary idea I've heard all year."

"Revolutionary how?" asked President Blackwood.

"Because it forces trust," she explained. "Not voluntary trust, or trust based on promises, but trust based on cryptographic evidence that can be verified by anyone, anywhere, at any time."

"Trust through transparency," mused President Dubois. "What a novel concept."

President Blackwood stood up and began pacing again. "You know what the really crazy thing is? This would actually work."

"What would work?" asked Prime Minister Pemberton.

"Implementing this globally. If every major economy adopted this system simultaneously, the competitive disadvantages would disappear because everyone would be operating under the same rules."

Chancellor Weissmann nodded slowly. "And the benefits would be universal. Reduced systemic risk, eliminated regulatory arbitrage, guaranteed transparency..."

"Built-in sustainability," added President Volkov.

"Architectural anti-fraud," noted Prime Minister Tanaka.

"And the end of regulatory capture," concluded President Blackwood.

Prime Minister Torrino threw up his hands. "I can't believe I'm saying this, but I'm starting to think our panicked assistant might have accidentally brought us the most important document in the history of international finance."

Prime Minister Bouchard looked thoughtful. "So instead of discussing the risks outlined in the World Stability Outlook 2030..."

"We're discussing the solution," finished President Dubois.

"A solution that eliminates the need for most traditional oversight," added Chancellor Weissmann.

"Because oversight becomes automatic," said President Volkov.

"And verifiable," added Prime Minister Pemberton.

"And impossible to corrupt," concluded President Blackwood.

I looked around the room at the most powerful economic leaders in the world, all of whom were now quietly reading through a technical monograph that had accidentally fallen into their hands.

"So," I said carefully, "what do we do now?"

President Blackwood looked up from the document. "Now? Now we figure out how to make this happen."

"All of it?" asked Prime Minister Torrino.

"All of it," confirmed President Volkov.

"And if other countries don't want to participate?" asked Prime Minister Tanaka.

"Then they don't get to participate in our financial systems," said Chancellor Weissmann matter-of-factly.

"Economic sovereignty through architectural transparency," mused President Dubois. "How beautifully ironic."

Prime Minister Pemberton closed her copy of the document. "This would require a complete restructuring of our regulatory frameworks."

"Yes," agreed President Blackwood. "But it would also eliminate most of our regulatory problems."

Prime Minister Bouchard was nodding slowly. "It's like the difference between trying to prevent crimes by having better detectives, versus designing a society where crimes can't be committed in the first place."

"Exactly!" I said. "It's prevention through architecture rather than detection through investigation."

Prime Minister Tanaka looked up from her reading. "The document mentions something about digital evidence standards. FRE 901/902 compliance."

"Federal Rules of Evidence," I explained. "This system would create digital evidence that meets the highest forensic standards."

"So every financial decision could be used as court evidence?" asked Prime Minister Torrino.

"Every financial decision would automatically be admissible as evidence," I confirmed.

President Volkov was reading something that made her eyes widen. "Smart Contract Treasury. Immutable, distributed control over regulatory enforcement mechanisms."

"Meaning?" asked President Blackwood.

"Meaning the enforcement itself becomes automated and unchangeable," explained President Volkov.

"Automated enforcement," repeated Chancellor Weissmann. "The regulator becomes a computer program."

"A computer program that cannot be bribed, threatened, or politically pressured," noted President Dubois.

"And cannot make mistakes due to fatigue, bias, or incompetence," added Prime Minister Pemberton.

President Blackwood sat back in his chair. "This is either the most brilliant idea in the history of financial regulation..."

"Or the most dangerous," finished Chancellor Weissmann.

"Why dangerous?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"Because it would work," said President Volkov. "And if it works, it would render most of our current economic institutions obsolete."

Prime Minister Torrino was shaking his head. "This is crazy. We're talking about replacing human judgment with mathematical certainty."

"Mathematical certainty that enforces human values," corrected Prime Minister Tanaka.

"And if the mathematical certainty is wrong?" pressed Prime Minister Torrino.

"Then the system pauses and escalates to human stewardship," I reminded him.

"Which means humans still make the final decisions," noted President Blackwood.

"After being presented with complete, verifiable evidence," added President Volkov.

Prime Minister Pemberton closed her eyes. "Do you realize what this would mean for economic policy? Instead of arguing about intentions and interpretations, we could argue about mathematical proofs."

"Arguments based on evidence rather than ideology," mused Chancellor Weissmann.

President Blackwood stood up and walked to the window, looking out over Lake Geneva. "You know what's the most revolutionary thing about this system?"

"What?" asked President Dubois.

"It assumes that the purpose of financial systems is to serve society, not to exploit society."

The room fell quiet.

"And it makes that assumption computationally enforceable," added Prime Minister Tanaka.

"Regardless of who's in charge," added President Volkov.

"Regardless of political pressure," added Chancellor Weissmann.

"Regardless of economic incentives," added Prime Minister Pemberton.

President Blackwood turned back to the room. "So we're agreed?"

"Agreed on what?" asked Prime Minister Torrino.

"That we need to figure out how to make this happen."

"Globally?" asked Prime Minister Bouchard.

"Globally," confirmed President Blackwood.

"And if other countries don't want to participate?" asked President Dubois.

"Then they don't get access to our markets," said Chancellor Weissmann matter-of-factly.

"Economic integration through architectural transparency," mused Prime Minister Tanaka.

"Trust through mathematical proof," added President Volkov.

President Blackwood looked around the room. "Any other questions?"

"Just one," said Prime Minister Pemberton. "How do we explain to our respective publics that we're implementing a global financial system based on a document that was accidentally delivered by a panicked aide who couldn't figure out color-coding?"

The room erupted in laughter.

President Volkov was wiping tears from her eyes. "We tell them the truth. That sometimes the most important solutions come from the most unexpected places."

"And that sometimes," added President Blackwood, "the best policy innovations come from admitting that we don't know everything."

"Or," suggested Chancellor Weissmann dryly, "we could just tell them that our aide has been promoted to Director of International Document Management."

That set off another round of laughter.

Prime Minister Torrino raised his hand. "Motion to formally request that our Chief Economic Strategist immediately begin drafting implementation proposals for this Ternary Logic framework."

"Seconded," said President Volkov.

"Any opposed?" asked Prime Minister Pemberton.

No one spoke.

"Motion carried," she announced. "Strategist, you have your marching orders."

I looked around the room at the leaders who, an hour ago, had been expecting a routine briefing on global economic risks, and who were now committed to implementing the most comprehensive financial governance system in human history.

One thing was certain: Marcus was definitely getting a promotion.

"And Strategist," added President Blackwood with a grin, "next time you prepare briefing materials, maybe double-check which document is which?"

"I'll make a note of that, sir," I said. "Though I have to admit, this mix-up might be the best mistake in the history of international diplomacy."

"Agreed," said President Dubois. "Now, who wants to explain to their respective finance ministers that we're about to revolutionize global economics based on a monograph about 'Evidentiary Triadic Frameworks'?"

The room fell silent as the reality of what they'd just committed to began to sink in.

Finally, Chancellor Weissmann spoke up: "I'll buy coffee for whoever volunteers to make that call."

Everyone quickly found other things to look at.

"Well," said President Blackwood, "I suppose that's why they pay us the big bucks."

As I sat there, watching the most powerful economic leaders in the world grapple with the implications of accidentally discovering the future of global governance, I couldn't help but smile. Sometimes the best solutions really do come from the most unexpected places.

And sometimes, just sometimes, a panicked aide with a color-coding problem can accidentally change the world.

The Ternary Logic revolution had officially begun.
