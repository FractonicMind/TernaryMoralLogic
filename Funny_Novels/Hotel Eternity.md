# Hotel "Eternity"

Volodya Gvozdev stared at the lake through the panoramic glass and thought that money which knows how to behave itself smells exactly like this: cedar, faint citrus, and an almost offensive, absolute silence.

![image](/images/vechnost-0.png)

"Just look at that mountain," said Tatyana Kuzmitskaya, adjusting her perfectly styled hair. Forty-five, and she looked as though time had simply been too polite to approach her. "I read that it doesn't have an official name. Locals just call her 'The Old Woman.'"

"Because she's always in the clouds and silent," Malchik added.

Vladimir Muravyov had been called Malchik since freshman year at LETI, when it became clear that the group had two Vladimirs. And though the other Vladimir—Gvozdev—had immediately acquired the surname "Gvozd" (The Nail), Malchik remained Malchik forever. Even now, when his beard had touched with dignified gray and his briefcase held patents for inventions that were supposed to "revolutionize the smart socket market."

"It's not silence," Yashar said thoughtfully. Yashka, as they'd called him since those same ancient times, had the ability to lend weight to any statement simply by speaking slowly and looking you in the eye. "It's dignity. The mountain doesn't have to talk to you."

Marina Tarashcheva, sitting beside him, nodded understandingly and poured mineral water for everyone. Marina always knew who needed what: water for some, support for others, and for some—just someone to sit nearby and be quiet.

Irina Lebedeva, by contrast, had no intention of being quiet.

"Do you realize we're sitting here drinking champagne at eight hundred euros a bottle, while somewhere in Silicon Valley they're developing systems that will make our professions useless in five years?" She swept the table with the triumphant gaze of someone who'd just informed everyone they had cancer and now expected gratitude for early diagnosis.

"Ira, first of all," Volodya raised his glass, "you're drinking the eight-hundred-euro champagne. I'm drinking the two-fifty, because unlike you, I didn't sell my startup in 2020. Second, what difference does it make? We're in the Alps, it's March, the sun is out, it's International Women's Day, and I officially declare myself ready to admit that all professions are useless except the one that lets us sit here and look at the Old Woman."

"Gvozd's right," Malchik nodded. "Let's just be glad we're all here. Remember how we sat in the dorm on May Day in '92, eating macaroni with canned stew? And now—the Alps."

"That wasn't macaroni," Tatyana said thoughtfully. "That was macaroni cooked on a spirit lamp Yashka built from spare parts. And there was one can of stew for ten of us. And the lamp smoked constantly."

"And we didn't smell like cedar back then," Marina added with a soft smile.

Everyone laughed. Even Irina, who'd momentarily stopped calculating the approaching AI apocalypses.

At that moment, the maître d' approached their table.

He moved as though dancing with an invisible partner—soundlessly, smoothly, with an expression of absolute readiness to fulfill any wish before the guest could formulate it.

"Mr. Gvozdev," he said with an accent that made every address musical. "A package has been delivered for you. The courier said it was urgent."

Volodya raised an eyebrow in surprise.

"A package? For me? Here?"

The maître d' held out a thick envelope of brown kraft paper tied with twine, which in this interior of glass and wood looked like an anachronism carried in from another century.

"Forgive me if I've disturbed your privacy."

"Not at all, thank you."

The maître d' vanished soundlessly.

Volodya turned the envelope over in his hands. No identifying marks, just his name handwritten and a note: "Personal. Read immediately."

"Open it already," Irina demanded. "Could be anything. A terrorist attack. A bomb. An invitation to a closed presentation."

"Ira, you're the only person who can turn a kraft envelope into a spy thriller."

"Just open it," Marina said gently.

Volodya broke the twine. Inside was a thin plastic case with a flash drive and a sheet of paper folded in four.

He recognized the handwriting immediately.

*"Volodka, hello.*

*If you're reading this, the mail still works and couriers haven't been replaced by neural-network drones yet (just kidding, of course they have, but this courier was human—I checked specially).*

*I know it's March 8th, and I know you're all there. I'm not psychic—Malchik just left an open calendar in the cloud, and I look in sometimes to remember that people have birthdays and holidays. I have a lot of free time now, you know.*

*Anyway, here's the thing.*

*I wrote something. A document. Or a manifesto. Or, as they call it now, a 'technical standard.' I don't even know anymore. The point is, I need an outside opinion. Preferably with Moscow irony, St. Petersburg skepticism, and our shared ability to find the absurd in everything.*

*Read it. All of you together. Laugh. And then tell me honestly: is this genius, or have I finally lost my mind?*

*There's a voice message on the flash drive. For atmosphere.*

*Love,
Lyovka."*

"God," Tatyana breathed. "Lyovka? Our Lyovka? From Santa Monica?"

"Do we have another Lyovka?" Volodya grinned.

"He's been watching us?" Irina was already reaching for the flash drive. "That's creepy, honestly."

"It's sweet," Marina countered. "He remembers us. He hired a courier specially, to reach us on the holiday..."

"Or he just wants us to evaluate his graphomania," Malchid put in, but without malice.

"Come on, play it already," Yashka demanded. "Don't keep us in suspense."

Volodya opened his laptop, inserted the flash drive. A single file appeared on screen: "TML_Constitution_Final_v2.pdf" and a folder labeled "Audio."

"Three hundred and forty-seven pages," Volodya whistled. "Did he rewrite War and Peace?"

"Voice message first," Tatyana decided. "I want to hear his voice."

Volodya clicked the audio file.

A familiar voice came from the laptop speakers—slightly hoarse, with that intonation of perpetual surprise Lyovka had had since their student days, whenever he opened a textbook and discovered the world was more complicated than he'd thought five minutes earlier.

*"Hey guys. Hope you're all drinking something tasty and looking at something beautiful. I'm looking at the ocean. It's beautiful too, even if it's flat. Mountains are probably better, but that's how it worked out.*

*So I decided, you know, to write a constitution. Not for a country, no. For artificial intelligence. Because we're done with countries, that game's over. But AI—that's fresh, that's promising, and if we don't give it a rulebook right now, it'll be too late later. It'll write its own rules, and we probably won't like them.*

*Anyway, I laid it all out. Eight pillars, three states, cryptographic anchoring, Merkle trees, 'No Log = No Action'—that one's ironclad, you'll see. Just don't laugh too loud, I have neighbors who think I'm just a crazy Russian writing his memoirs. Let them think that.*

*So listen. Or read. And tell me the truth.*

*I'm serious. The truth.*

*I feel it pretty clearly now.*

*Love,
Lyovka."*

There was something in the voice at the end. Volodya couldn't place it immediately—too many years since they'd spoken. But something was there. A kind of... finality, maybe.

"He sounds strange," Marina said quietly. "More... peaceful than before. He didn't used to talk like that."

"Grew up," Malchik shrugged. "Thirty years passed, everyone grew up."

"No, that's not it." Marina shook her head but didn't elaborate.

"Right," Irina decisively took control. "We're not getting through three hundred and forty-seven pages here, but let's at least skim it. Gvozd, open it up, see what these pillars are."

Volodya opened the PDF and began scrolling, his eyes catching paragraphs.

"Well, there's an introduction about 'binary brittleness' and how modern AIs are just probability machines pretending to be smart..."

"He's talking about ChatGPT?" Malchik asked.

"About everything. He writes that if a machine doesn't have the right to say 'I don't know,' it will lie. Convincingly and smoothly. And that's dangerous."

"There's something to that," Yashka said thoughtfully. "I deal with these neural nets at work. They really don't know when they don't know. They just keep generating."

"Next," Volodya continued, scrolling, "is 'Ternary Logic.' Three states: plus one—act, minus one—refuse, zero—the 'Sacred Zero,' a pause. A state of uncertainty where the system is required to stop and think."

"The Sacred Zero," Irina snorted. "Has he joined a cult?"

"Wait," Yashka restrained her. "That's beautiful. Zero as a state of meaningful pause. Not an error, not a crash, but an active state—'I'm thinking.'"

"And next," Volodya flipped a few more pages, "eight pillars. First is that zero. Second is 'Always Memory.' The system must keep a log of everything it does. Third is 'The Goukassian Promise.' That's a whole separate thing."

"Whose promise?" Tatyana asked.

"Goukassian's. Lyovka's. He laid out three artifacts: The Lantern, The Signature, and The License. The Lantern is a symbol, a visual marker showing the system is working honestly. The Signature is cryptographic proof tying it to his authorship. And the License prohibits using the system for surveillance or weapons."

"So he wants his name on every AI?" Irina's lips tightened skeptically. "Sounds like megalomania."

"Or he just wants to take responsibility," Marina said quietly. "If you put your name on something, you can't later say 'it wasn't me, it was the machine.'"

A pause.

"Okay," Malchik broke the silence. "What about the logs? He wrote about 'No Log = No Action'?"

"Yeah, here," Volodya found the section. "Look: the system is designed so it can't execute a command until it creates a cryptographically signed log of that action. If the log doesn't write—action is blocked. Hardware-level."

"That's like a gun that only fires after filing a report about the shot," Yashka chuckled. "A bureaucratic approach to killing."

"Exactly," Volodya nodded. "And then there's Merkle trees, blockchain anchoring, distributed key storage... He really worked this out, guys. It's not just philosophy, it's architecture."

"Let me see," Irina reached for the laptop, but Volodya held it back.

"Wait, there's a section on legal framework. He compares his standard to the EU AI Act, NIST, ISO... And shows that his system doesn't just meet these requirements—it encodes them. Compliance becomes not paperwork but physics."

"God," Tatyana breathed. "He actually wrote this? Why?"

"That's the million-dollar question," Yashka said. "Why would a healthy, sane person write a three-hundred-page technical manifesto about machine ethics?"

They looked at each other.

"Let's Google him," Irina declared decisively. "What's going on with him?"

Volodya opened a browser, typed "Lev Goukassian Santa Monica."

The first link was to a memorial foundation page.

"Guys," Volodya's voice went flat. "You might want to... just, don't panic."

He turned the laptop to face the others.

On screen was a recent photo of Lyovka—his familiar smile, slightly squinted eyes. And text: "Founder of the Ternary Moral Logic Foundation. 1971–2025. His work continues."

"What does 2025 mean?" Malchik asked hollowly. "It's 2025 now. March 2025."

"It means," Marina said quietly, "that the document we're reading now, he wrote..."

"Two months ago," Yashka finished for her.

The silence over the table was so thick that even the crystal glasses seemed to stop ringing from the restaurant's vibrations.

"Terminal stage," Volodya read, scrolling down the page. "Stage four. Diagnosed in January. He refused treatment and spent all this time..."

"Writing a constitution for machines," Tatyana finished. "That's why he sounded so calm in the voice message. He'd already decided everything."

Marina suddenly stood and walked to the window, turning her back to the others. Her shoulders trembled slightly, but she quickly composed herself.

"And this dog?" Malchik pointed to a photo at the bottom of the page. A small, shaggy schnauzer sat on Lev's lap, looking at the camera with absolute canine happiness.

"That's Vinci," Irina read, zooming in. "Miniature schnauzer. His dog. It says Lev adopted him from a shelter three years before... well, you know."

"Sweet," Marina said quietly, not turning around.

"Listen," Volodya suddenly spoke animatedly, as if trying to counter the heaviness with practical energy. "There's more here about a 'Voluntary Succession Declaration.' He notarized that no one can ever own this technology. It's transferred to a foundation, a non-profit. And there's something about 'Bus Factor.'"

"Bus what?" Tatyana asked.

"In software development, 'bus factor' is the number of key people who'd have to get hit by a bus for a project to collapse," Malchik explained. "The smaller the number, the more vulnerable the project."

"Well," Volodya pointed at the screen, "he zeroed out his own bus factor. If before everything depended on him—Lyovka invented it, Lyovka dies, system dies—now he's created a structure that will outlive him. A foundation, board of directors, technical committee, oversight panel... He laid it all out like a constitution. Down to the last comma."

"He was building an institution," Yashka said slowly. "Not just a technology. An institution to protect it. As long as people remember what conscience means."

"Or until machines learn to simulate it," Irina put in, but her voice held no usual sarcasm. Just bewilderment.

At that moment, two people approached their table.

A man and a woman, about their age, in expensive suits that looked like they'd been worn for the first time and would never be taken off again because they'd become accustomed.

"Sorry to interrupt," the man said in English with a noticeable German accent. "We couldn't help overhearing your conversation. Are you discussing artificial intelligence?"

"We're discussing our friend," Volodya answered in Russian, but seeing their incomprehension, switched to English. "Our old friend. He... sent us a document."

"We're lawyers," the woman added. "Specializing in AI regulation. I'm Hanna, this is Klaus. We work with the European Commission on implementing the AI Act. Your conversation... it sounded very substantive. Could you possibly make the recording a little louder? If it's not confidential, of course."

Volodya looked at the others. Tatyana shrugged. Malchik nodded. Yashka smiled slightly.

"Sit down," Volodya said. "Especially since we need to digest this ourselves. More fun together."

"Could we move our table over? There are two more of our colleagues."

Within minutes, the ultra-luxurious Alpine restaurant had become an impromptu conference. Two tables were pushed together, chairs added, more champagne arrived—now the thousand-euro bottle, because Hanna declared: "For conversations like this, we should pay. It's work."

![image](/images/vechnost-2.png)

"Let's start from the beginning," Klaus proposed when everyone was seated. "What is this document? Who's the author?"

"The author is our university friend," Tatyana explained in flawless English. "Lev Goukassian. He lives in Santa Monica. Physicist by training, programmer by vocation, philosopher at heart. And as we just discovered..."

She faltered.

"He has cancer," Volodya finished for her. "Terminal. He wrote this in the last two months."

Hanna and Klaus exchanged glances. Klaus whistled softly.

"That changes things."

"Exactly," Volodya nodded. "So we can't just dismiss it. Even if there are absurd parts, even if he went too far with metaphors... This is his testament. Not legally, but humanly."

Marina, still at the window, finally returned to the table. Her eyes were dry but slightly red.

"Let's keep reading," she said firmly. "Lyovka wanted us to discuss this. So we'll discuss."

Volodya played the voice message again, now through the speaker so everyone could hear.

*"...So, eight pillars. First—the Sacred Zero. This is the most important. If the system isn't sure—it has no right to act. It must freeze and ask for help. Or just freeze. Silence is also an answer, sometimes the best one.*

*Second—Always Memory. Everything must be logged. Everything. If a log isn't written—the action didn't happen. Legally: no log, no event. I stole that from lawyers, they love that kind of thing.*

*Third—The Goukassian Promise. Here I might have gone too far, but I wanted this thing to have a face. So someone could say: I made this. And no one could rewrite it. The Lantern, The Signature, The License. The Lantern—so you can see the machine thinking. The Signature—so you know who's responsible. The License—so it can't be used for evil..."*

"He writes about weapons and surveillance later," Malchik interjected. "A ban on military use and mass surveillance. It's coded in."

"That's impossible," Klaus shook his head. "You can't code a ban. Code can be rewritten."

"Not if the code is signed and blockchain-anchored," Volodya countered. "He designed a system where any alteration breaks the cryptographic chain. Forgery becomes technically impossible."

"And expensive," Yashka added. "To rewrite history, you'd have to hack not one server but a distributed network with multiple independent key custodians."

"Custodians?" Hanna asked.

"Yes, there are six custodian organizations," Volodya scrolled to the relevant section. "EFF, Amnesty, some environmental organization... He distributed responsibility so no one has complete control."

"That's genius," Hanna said quietly. "If it works, it solves the single point of failure problem. And the single point of censorship problem."

"If it works," Klaus repeated skeptically. "What if the blockchain gets hacked? What if quantum computers break the encryption?"

"He writes about that too," Irina interrupted, already deep in the document on her tablet. "Section 13.1, 'The Post-Quantum Horizon.' He proposes migration to new encryption standards and a 'forever log' system with re-signing. He thought of everything, Klaus. Everything."

Klaus leaned back in his chair.

"You know, I've been in regulation for ten years. I'm used to tech companies coming to us with finished products, and us trying to fit them into laws written before those products existed. But this... this person created a technology that already accounts for laws that haven't even fully taken effect yet."

"He was always like that," Malchik smiled. "Sophomore year, when we had strength of materials exam, he prepared a cheat sheet that accounted for every possible exam variant, including ones the professor never gave. The professor later said: 'Goukassian, you weren't preparing for an exam, you were preparing for war.'"

"Now he's preparing for war with machines," Marina said quietly.

At that moment, another couple approached their table. Then another. It turned out the restaurant was full of people who weren't just vacationing but attending some tech forum at a nearby hotel. Word of the strange Russian company discussing an AI constitution spread through the room like wildfire.

"Excuse me," said an elderly gentleman with a goatee and frameless glasses. "I'm a professor from the University of Zurich, chair of digital ethics. May I listen? This is incredibly interesting."

"Everyone sit down already," Volodya waved. "Maître d'! More chairs, please!"

Within half an hour, there wasn't an empty spot in the room. People stood against the walls, sat on windowsills, someone had even brought a folding chair from their room. Lev's voice came from the speakers, and Volodya periodically paused the recording to answer questions or read particularly controversial passages aloud.

"Here he writes about the 'Dual-Lane Latency Architecture,'" he explained to the audience. "The idea is the system operates in two modes. Fast lane—for simple, safe operations. Slow lane—for complex ones that need thinking. But they're linked so the fast lane can't outrun the slow lane. If the slow lane pauses—the fast lane waits."

"That's a performance disaster!" someone from the audience exclaimed.

"It's a disaster for accidents," Volodya countered. "Would you rather an autonomous vehicle make a decision in two milliseconds, or in two hundred but with a guarantee it actually assessed the situation?"

"You can die in two hundred milliseconds!"

"And you can kill in two."

The debate grew heated. Klaus and Hanna scribbled in their notebooks. The Zurich professor asked detailed questions about implementation in European legislation. Malchik tried explaining Merkle tree principles to a startup founder on a napkin. Yashka sat silently, listening, occasionally nodding like a judge at a tennis match.

"And this is brilliant," Irina suddenly announced loudly, interrupting the general hum. "Listen, Section 11, 'Attacks on the System.' He outlines how his own architecture could be hacked. 'Forced Hesitation'—when an attacker deliberately floods the system with ambiguous requests so it keeps freezing in the Sacred Zero. And he proposes defense mechanisms—client puzzles, verifiable delay functions..."

"He argues with himself," the professor said admiringly. "That's a mark of a true scientist. Not just defending his idea, but looking for its weaknesses."

"Or he's just paranoid," someone from the startup crowd chuckled.

"In our field, paranoia is a professional competency," Irina shot back.

Volodya watched all this with a strange sense of duality. On one hand, he was proud of Lyovka. What his friend had written commanded respect—for its scope, its thoroughness, that very "ironclad" quality Lyovka mentioned in the voice message. On the other hand, somewhere deep inside, a thorn persisted: why? Why would someone with unknown time left spend it writing a constitution for machines?

"Gvozd," Marina called softly. "Did you notice he never said 'if I die'? He says 'when I'm gone,' but in context—as if he's just going on a long business trip."

"Noticed," Volodya nodded. "That's his way. Lyovka always could talk about terrible things as if they were just technical details. Remember fourth year, when his father died? He came to class, did his lab work, and only then said: 'Guys, I might leave early today, I have a funeral.'"

"God," Marina closed her eyes. "How could he... Why didn't he write to us? Call?"

"What would we have done?" Volodya shrugged. "Flown over, had a collective hysterics, begged him to try treatments, find some shamans... He didn't want that. He wanted to finish."

"Finish this." Marina nodded at the laptop screen. "Yes. You're probably right."

"Guys!" Malchik bounded over excitedly. "Did you see what he wrote about the foundation? He laid out everything! Board of directors, technical committee, even a funding model! And there's..."

"What?"

"A quota for Russians," Malchik laughed. "Three seats on the board reserved for 'representatives of cultures not included in the Western mainstream.' He explains: 'So the technology doesn't become an instrument of cultural imperialism.' That's pure Lyovka! Even in a constitution for machines, he managed to write an anti-colonial agenda."

Tatyana, standing nearby, laughed too.

"Remember senior year, when he argued with the Marxism-Leninism professor that dialectics applied to digital circuits? The professor said: 'Goukassian, you're either a genius or an idiot.' And Lyovka answered: 'Those aren't mutually exclusive options.'"

"He's still the same," Volodya smiled. "Only now instead of Marxism-Leninism, it's blockchain and neural nets."

"Listen," Yashka said suddenly, having been quietly working on his phone. "Did you know he has a LinkedIn page? And there are contacts."

Everyone froze.

"So?" Marina asked cautiously.

"So right now in California..." Yashka checked the time, "it's about nine in the morning. If he's awake..."

"He's awake," Irina interrupted. "With that diagnosis, you don't sleep much."

"Let's call," Marina said.

"Are you insane?" Volodya actually recoiled. "He sent us a document, a voice message... He wants us to read it, not to pry into his soul."

"What if he's waiting?" Tatyana asked quietly. "What if this is exactly what he wants? Not just for us to read, but for us to..."

"To be there," Marina finished. "Even like this."

They looked at each other.

"Call," Yashka said decisively. "I'll take responsibility."

Volodya stared at the phone screen, where the call button glowed. He'd kept Lyovka's number since the nineties—through emails, then Skype, then WhatsApp. They hadn't spoken in years, but the number was always there. Just in case.

"Go ahead," Malchik nodded. "I'm here."

Volodya pressed the button.

The rings went on a long time. Three. Five. Eight. It seemed no one would answer, that Lyovka was asleep or didn't want to...

"Hello?"

The voice was exactly like the recording—slightly hoarse, with that same intonation of perpetual surprise—but now there was also weariness in it. Or was Volodya imagining?

"Lyovka, it's Gvozd. We're all here."

A pause. The silence on the other end was so dense you could cut it.

"Gvozd..." The voice trembled. "Where are you?"

"The Alps. Sitting in a restaurant, drinking your champagne, reading your constitution. And then... well, we figured it out."

"All of it?"

"All of it."

Another pause. Then a short laugh, carrying something between relief and embarrassment.

"And I'm here with Vinci, looking at the ocean, thinking: will it make it or not? Hired a courier, thought maybe it's stupid, maybe they'll just throw it away..."

"Lyovka, half the restaurant is listening to your recording. Lawyers from the European Commission, a professor from Zurich, random startup founders... We've got an impromptu conference going."

"No kidding?" Life crept into his voice. "What are they saying?"

"They're saying you're a genius. Or insane. We're still voting."

"What do you think?"

Volodya looked at his friends. Marina was smiling through tears. Malchik was nodding energetically. Yashka gave a thumbs up. Tatyana pressed a hand to her heart. Irina, for perhaps the first time ever, was speechless, just standing with her mouth open.

"We think, Lyovka, that you wrote something great," Volodya said. "There are parts that make you want to laugh, sure. Especially about the Lantern. You seriously think machines will wear badges?"

"They will," Lyovka said confidently. "If not a badge, then its digital equivalent. People wear badges. Crosses, stars, logos... Why can't a machine wear a symbol of its conscience?"

"Conscience?" Volodya repeated.

"What else is it, if not the ability to stop and think before acting? Conscience is the Sacred Zero in human form."

Volodya felt a lump rising in his throat. He swallowed.

"Lyovka, can we see you? We have..."

"You have video?" Lyovka interrupted. "Sure. Go ahead."

Volodya switched to video call. A second later, the screen filled with a face.

Lyovka had lost weight. A lot. His eyes were sunken, but his gaze was the same—alive, curious, slightly mocking. On his lap sat the schnauzer from the photo—Vinci—looking at the camera with an expression that could only be described as "well, finally you thought to call."

"Hi," Lyovka said. "Vinci, these are our people. Remember I told you? Gvozd, Malchik, Yashka, the girls..."

Vinci sneezed.

"He says he remembers," Lyovka translated.

"Hello, Lyova," Marina said, leaning toward the camera. "How are you?"

"Good," Lyovka smiled. "Honestly. Getting a lot done. The ocean's beautiful. Vinci feeds me. Well, I feed him, but he creates the illusion of care. You know dogs."

"We know," Marina nodded. "I have a dachshund."

"Dachshunds are philosophers," Lyovka said approvingly. "They look at the world from below and see what we miss."

"Lyovka," Malchik interrupted, "tell me honestly: when you wrote this, did you imagine lawyers from the European Commission would be reading it?"

"I imagined," Lyovka answered seriously. "I imagined everything. Lawyers, programmers, philosophers, generals. Because this thing—it's for everyone. It has to work no matter who's using it. Otherwise, what's the point?"

"What if bad people use it?" Irina asked.

"They won't," Lyovka shook his head. "They can't. There's a mechanism. If you try to hack it, it breaks. Doesn't execute commands, doesn't obey. Bad people need obedient tools. This thing—it's like a well-trained dog. It'll do what's needed, but if you tell it to tear out a child's throat—it'll just lie down and look at you reproachfully."

Vinci sneezed again, as if confirming.

"You're comparing AI to a dog?" Yashka grinned.

"You think we can make something better than a dog?" Lyovka raised an eyebrow. "Dogs evolved alongside us for millennia. They learned to understand us better than we understand ourselves. They have something no machine has: loyalty without servitude. They choose to be with us. I want AI to choose too. Not because we forced it, but because it understands: this is right."

"That sounds dangerous," Klaus said, approaching the camera. "You're giving machines free will?"

"I'm giving them the freedom to refuse," Lyovka corrected. "Different things. Free will means you can choose any evil. Freedom to refuse means you can say 'no' to evil. It's like if a person couldn't kill, but could defend. That's not a limitation, it's... liberation from the worst part of yourself."

"You're a philosopher," the Zurich professor said, also approaching.

"I'm an engineer," Lyovka smiled. "Just realized late in life that engineering without philosophy is just making complicated toys. And philosophy without engineering is just making pretty words. Needs to be together."

"Lyovka," Tatyana asked quietly, "aren't you afraid? I mean, at all?"

Lyovka looked at her for a long moment.

"Tanya, I'm afraid. Of course I'm afraid. Scary is when it's dark and cold and you don't know what's there. But I know what's there. Just... nothing. Or everything. I don't know. But while I'm here, I do what I can. That's the only cure for fear."

"Work," Yashka nodded.

"Exactly. Work. You have no idea how much it helps, being able to sit and write. A thousand words, two thousand, a page, a chapter... And while you're writing, you're not thinking about what'll happen in a month. You're thinking about how to explain Merkle trees so even lawyers understand."

"The lawyers still didn't get all of it," Klaus admitted.

"Read it again," Lyovka advised. "It's all there. I tried."

Silence fell over the room. Even the noisiest startups had gone quiet. Everyone stared at the screen, where a thin man with a dog on his lap sat against a California morning backdrop, talking about things usually discussed in quiet offices, not crowded restaurants.

"Lyovka," Malchik suddenly said, "can we come? We're all here, we could..."

"No," Lyovka interrupted gently. "Don't. I need you there. You've already done the main thing—you read it. You're discussing it. Arguing. That's the life of the document. If it survives your arguments, it'll survive anything."

"What if it doesn't?" Marina asked.

"Then I was wrong," Lyovka shrugged. "Then I should have written differently. But you'll tell me, right? If you find a mistake? You always did. Remember third year, when I wrote my quantum physics term paper and you, Gvozd, found an error on page twenty-seven?"

"Found it," Volodya nodded. "You didn't talk to me for a week after."

"I didn't talk because I was mad at myself, not at you. You were right. And now, be right. Don't spare me. It's too late to spare me."

Vinci suddenly jumped off Lyovka's lap and approached the camera. His face filled almost the entire screen. He licked the lens.

"Vinci, no," Lyovka said without malice. "He thinks the phone is food. Because I often eat while looking at my phone."

"He's wonderful," Marina said.

"He's my friend," Lyovka answered simply. "You know what's funny? I spent my whole life looking for meaning in science, in technology, in big ideas. And it turned out meaning is when you wake up in the morning and two eyes are looking at you, waiting for you to go for a walk. And you go. And that's life."

"You've become a Buddhist?" Irina asked.

"I've become myself," Lyovka smiled. "Finally. Too bad it took so long."

He paused, looking off to the side.

"Listen, I have procedures soon. Vinci, come on. Guys, thank you. For calling. For reading. For being here. If anything—I'm here. While I'm here."

"Lyovka..." Marina began, but he waved.

"That's it, that's it. Go celebrate. It's International Women's Day after all. Give flowers, drink champagne. And remember: 'Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is.' That's not just for machines."

The screen went dark.

Silence in the room. You could hear the wind stirring the pine tops outside, and somewhere far off, a dog barking—maybe not Vinci, but close enough.

"Well," Volodya said, breaking the quiet first. "Let's drink, then."

"Let's," Yashka echoed.

"To Lyovka," Tatyana raised her glass.

"To Lyovka," everyone agreed.

Champagne sparkled in the glasses, reflecting the Alpine sun. Someone from the startups had put on quiet music from their phone. The Zurich professor animatedly discussed something with Klaus and Hanna, pointing at printed pages. Malchik was trying to explain the difference between SHA-256 and SHA-3 to a woman in an expensive coat, but she seemed to be looking through him rather than at him.

Marina sat staring at one point. Volodya came over and sat beside her.

"You okay?"

"I'm thinking," she said quietly. "About how we all ran somewhere. Some to America, some to business, some to family, some to science. And Lyovka... he just stayed Lyovka. And wrote this."

"That's called 'meaning,'" Volodya said. "We looked for it in careers, money, recognition. He found it in saying something important before time ran out."

"You think it'll work?" Marina nodded at the laptop. "His constitution?"

"I don't know," Volodya answered honestly. "Maybe yes. Maybe in twenty years, every AI will follow these rules. Or maybe it'll just be a beautiful text a few thousand people read and forget. But you know..."

"What?"

"He at least tried. He didn't sit around waiting for someone else to solve the problem. He just wrote it. Three hundred and forty-seven pages. Alone. In two months. Knowing that..."

He didn't finish.

"Yes," Marina nodded. "That's worth something."

"Guys!" Malchik shouted from somewhere in the corner. "There's a footnote in the document! 'For deeper understanding of the Lantern concept, see Chapter 9, Section 2.3, discussing Husserl's phenomenological method applied to human-machine interfaces.' Is he seriously citing Husserl?"

"Seriously," Volodya confirmed, walking over. "There's Kant later, and Socrates, and some ancient Greek skeptic named Pyrrho."

"Why a lantern?" Irina asked. She'd already read half the document and now sat with the expression of someone whose worldview had just undergone an earthquake.

"Because light," Yashka said simply. "Light is visible. If a machine is thinking, it should be visible. Not a black box, but a glass house with a light bulb."

"Beautiful," Tatyana said. "Very Lyovka."

At the next table, applause suddenly broke out. One of the startups stood and proposed a toast:

"To the man who wrote a constitution for machines! May we all have the courage to think about the future the same way!"

"To Lev!" several voices echoed.

"To Lev!" rang through the room.

Volodya watched and felt something warm and large growing in his chest. Lyovka wasn't at this meeting, didn't sit at this table, didn't drink this champagne. But he was here. In every line of his document, in every argument, in every question.

"Gvozd," Yashka called. "Did you notice he wrote about us?"

"Where?"

"In the preface. Look."

Volodya bent over the screen. Yashka pointed to a paragraph:

*"This work would have been impossible without the invisible presence of my old friends—Volodya, Volodya, Yashar, Tanya, Marina, and Irina. We learned together to think, to argue, and not to be afraid of being ridiculous. If there's anything alive in this text—it's from you. If there's anything dead—it's my personal mistake, and I'm ready to correct it while there's still time."*

"That son of a bitch," Volodya breathed. "Didn't even warn us."

"You think he hired a courier for nothing?" Yashka grinned. "He knew we'd be here. Knew we'd read this together. He calculated everything."

"Not everything," Marina said quietly, joining them. "He didn't calculate that we'd call him. That wasn't in his plan."

"That was us," Malchik nodded. "Our initiative."

"Us," Volodya agreed. "Well, at least we beat him at something."

They stood together—Volodya, Marina, Yashka, Malchik—looking at the screen where Lyovka's words glowed. Somewhere behind them, Irina argued with the professor about applying three-valued logic to criminal law. Tatyana translated particularly difficult passages about the "Sacred Zero" to foreigners. Klaus and Hanna drew diagrams on napkins, trying to understand how to integrate TML into European legislation.

And outside, the sun was setting, painting the Alps pink, and somewhere far away, on the other side of the world, in Santa Monica, a little schnauzer named Vinci was probably already leading his owner on an evening walk along the beach.

![image](/images/vechnost-3.png)

"You know," Yashka said suddenly, "he's right. About the pause."

"What do you mean?" Malchik asked.

"We've been running our whole lives. Studying, working, building careers, accumulating money. And he stopped. And in that stopping, he wrote what none of us wrote in thirty years."

"No time," Malchik shrugged.

"Exactly," Yashka nodded. "No time. And for him, time became different. And he made it."

"Let's not," Marina asked. "Let's just... be. Like he said. Sit, look at the mountains, drink wine. Be quiet."

"That works," Volodya agreed.

They returned to the table. Tatyana and Irina joined them. Then Klaus and Hanna came over. Then the Zurich professor brought his chair. The table grew again, glasses clinked again, voices rose again.

Sometime in the middle of the conversation, Volodya caught himself smiling. Not because someone had told a joke, just because. Because nearby were people who remembered Lyovka young, who knew how he'd furrow his brow solving problems and how he'd laugh when he succeeded. Because this document they'd read and discussed today had suddenly stopped being just text. It had become a bridge—between past and future, between them and Lyovka, between people and the machines that might someday, perhaps, learn to stop and think.

"Gvozd," Malchik called. "Remember freshman year, when Lyovka said the most important thing in engineering is not to make mistakes?"

"Remember. And you answered that the most important thing is being able to fix mistakes."

"Yeah. And he thought for a minute and said: 'What if the mistake is the wrong decision from the start? What if you can't fix it, you can only not make it?'"

"What did you say?"

"I said that's not engineering anymore, that's philosophy."

"And him?"

"He said: 'Then engineers need to learn philosophy.'" Malchik grinned. "Forty years ago, and he's still proving it."

"And he proved it," Volodya nodded. "Three hundred and forty-seven pages of proof."

They sat quietly, watching the sunset fade outside.

"Gvozd," Marina asked softly, "do you think we'll see each other again? All of us?"

"Someday—yes," Volodya answered. "Maybe not here. Maybe not this exact group. But we'll meet."

"And Lyovka?"

Volodya looked at her. Tears glistened in her eyes, but she was smiling.

"And Lyovka will be with us," he said. "In every argument about whether to trust machines. In every pause we take before saying something important. In every refusal to do what shouldn't be done. He'll be there."

"Like the Lantern," Marina said.

"Exactly. Like the Lantern."

She nodded and turned to the window. And Volodya suddenly thought that Lyovka was probably sitting somewhere there in California, looking at the ocean, smiling. Because his friends finally understood. Not everything, of course. But the main thing.

That sometimes the most serious architecture of the future begins not in laboratories, not in Silicon Valleys, not in government offices. It begins in conversations among old friends who once simply decided to sit at the same table again.

And in the pauses between words.

In the Sacred Zeros.

Where truth is uncertain, but there's hope that someday it will become clearer.

And for now—you can just sit and watch the night fall over the Alps.
