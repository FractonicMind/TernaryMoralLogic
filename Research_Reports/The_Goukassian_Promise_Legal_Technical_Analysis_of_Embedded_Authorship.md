The history of Pretty Good Privacy (PGP) offers a compelling and highly relevant precedent for understanding attribution in foundational cryptographic and governance frameworks. #### 3.1.1. Phil Zimmermann and the Creation of PGP

Phil Zimmermann's creation of PGP in 1991 was a watershed moment in the history of public-key cryptography. As a peace and political activist, Zimmermann was driven by a specific social need: to provide a tool for secure communication that could be used by activists, journalists, and other individuals handling sensitive information . His goal was to democratize privacy, putting the power of encryption into the hands of the public rather than leaving it solely in the domain of governments and large corporations. This mission is encapsulated in his own words: **"PGP empowers people to take their privacy into their own hands. There’s a growing social need for it. That’s why I wrote it"** . The initial release of PGP was a free, open-source software package, which facilitated its rapid, worldwide dissemination. This act of making the tool freely available was a direct challenge to the U.S. government's attempts to control cryptographic technology, as seen in the 1991 Senate Bill 266, which sought to mandate government access to plaintext communications . Zimmermann's defiance and the subsequent legal persecution he endured, including a criminal investigation that lasted from 1993 to 1996, transformed him from a mere software developer into a figurehead for the digital privacy movement . This narrative is crucial because it illustrates how a creator's identity, motivations, and struggles can become intrinsically linked to the framework they create, establishing a form of moral and historical attribution that persists regardless of the technical specifications of the software itself.

#### 3.1.2. Attribution in PGP: The Web of Trust and Public Key Certificates

Unlike TML, the PGP standard does not embed Phil Zimmermann's name or a unique identifier directly into its core algorithms or message formats. Instead, attribution and trust in PGP are managed through a decentralized model known as the **"Web of Trust."** This system relies on the use of public key certificates, which are essentially digital documents that bind a public key to an identity . A PGP certificate is more than just a public key; it is a collection of information that includes the owner's name, email address, and, crucially, a series of digital signatures from other users . These signatures serve as attestations, with each signer vouching for the authenticity of the binding between the public key and the identity it claims to represent. The strength of this model lies in its distributed nature; trust is not vested in a single central authority but is built up through a network of peer-to-peer endorsements.

A unique feature of PGP certificates is the self-signature, where the key owner signs their own key to assert their identity . Furthermore, a single certificate can accumulate multiple signatures from different individuals, creating a rich tapestry of trust relationships. For example, the public key certificate for Phil Zimmermann himself contains numerous signatures from other users who have met him and verified his identity, thereby attesting to the authenticity of his key . This mechanism provides a robust and flexible way to establish provenance and authenticity without hard-coding any individual's name into the protocol. It is a system of indirect but powerful attribution, where the creator's identity is preserved and verified through the collective actions of the community that uses the framework. This contrasts sharply with TML's approach of embedding a persistent, machine-readable identifier (ORCID) directly into its architecture, representing a different philosophical choice in balancing decentralization with the need for a clear, unambiguous point of origin.

### 3.2. Constitutional Frameworks: The U.S. Constitution

The U.S. Constitution, as a foundational legal and governance framework, offers a different set of precedents for how authorship and attribution are handled in long-lasting institutions. Unlike a piece of software or a cryptographic standard, the Constitution is a legal document that establishes the structure of a government and the rights of its citizens. The process of its creation and ratification was a collective effort, involving a group of individuals known as the "Framers." While the document itself does not list the names of these individuals, their identities are well-known and are a subject of extensive historical study. The case of the Constitution highlights the tension between the idea of a collective, anonymous creation and the need to preserve the memory of the individuals who were responsible for it. This provides a useful lens for thinking about the role of attribution in foundational frameworks, particularly in cases where the framework is intended to be a long-term, evolving institution.

#### 3.2.1. The Framers and "We the People"

The U.S. Constitution famously begins with the words **"We the People,"** a phrase that emphasizes the collective and democratic nature of the document's authority. This opening statement positions the Constitution as a creation of the people themselves, rather than a gift from a monarch or a small group of elites. This is a powerful rhetorical and political move, which has helped to legitimize the Constitution and ensure its long-term stability. However, this collective framing also serves to obscure the identities of the specific individuals who were most directly involved in its drafting. While the names of the Framers are well-known to historians and legal scholars, they are not a part of the document itself. This creates a distinction between the public, collective identity of the Constitution ("We the People") and the private, individual identities of its creators. This distinction is a key feature of the American constitutional tradition, and it has shaped how the document is interpreted and understood.

#### 3.2.2. Formal Recognition: The Signing of the Constitution

While the text of the Constitution does not name its authors, the process of its creation did include a formal act of attribution: the signing of the document by the delegates to the Constitutional Convention. This act of signing was a crucial moment of formal recognition, where the individuals who had participated in the drafting process put their names to the final product. The signatures served as a public declaration of support for the document and a commitment to its principles. In this sense, the signing of the Constitution can be seen as a form of embedded attribution, albeit one that is separate from the text of the document itself. The signatures are a permanent record of the individuals who were present at the creation of the new government, and they serve as a historical anchor for the institution. #### 3.2.3. Institutional Memory vs. Embedded Textual Attribution

The U.S. Constitution's approach to attribution can be characterized as a model of **institutional memory** rather than embedded textual attribution. The names of the framers are not included in the document's articles, which focus on establishing the structure and powers of the government. Instead, the identity of the originators is preserved through a variety of external mechanisms, including the signed parchment, historical records, and the ongoing interpretive work of scholars, lawyers, and judges. This approach has several advantages. It allows the Constitution to maintain a sense of timelessness and universality, as its authority is not tied to the personal reputations of its creators. It also creates a system of "living constitutionalism," where the meaning of the document can evolve over time through interpretation and amendment, without being constrained by the specific intentions of the framers. However, this approach also has its drawbacks. The lack of embedded attribution can sometimes lead to a sense of detachment from the historical context of the Constitution's creation, and it can make it more difficult to hold the framers accountable for their decisions.

The concept of **"original intent"** is a central and often controversial aspect of American constitutional law. Judges and scholars frequently debate the extent to which the original intentions of the framers should guide the interpretation of the Constitution . This debate highlights the importance of historical attribution, as it is impossible to discuss original intent without a clear understanding of who the framers were and what they believed. The extensive records of the Constitutional Convention, including James Madison's detailed notes, provide a rich source of information for this purpose . These records allow us to reconstruct the debates and compromises that led to the final document, and they provide a basis for evaluating the claims of originalism. The preservation of this institutional memory is essential for the legitimacy of the constitutional system. It ensures that the Constitution is not seen as a mysterious or arbitrary document, but rather as the product of a specific historical process, created by real people with real motivations. This model of institutional memory offers a valuable point of comparison for the Ternary Moral Logic framework. While TML has chosen to embed the Goukassian Promise and ORCID directly into its architecture, the U.S. Constitution demonstrates that a system of external attribution can also be effective in preserving the identity and accountability of a framework's originators. ### 4.2. ORCID vs. Digital Signatures and Trusted Timestamps

Digital signatures and trusted timestamps are two of the most fundamental technologies for establishing provenance and integrity in the digital realm. They are the workhorses of secure communication and audit trails, providing cryptographic proof of who did what and when. A digital signature, created using a private key, provides non-repudiable proof of authorship and ensures that a document has not been tampered with since it was signed. A trusted timestamp, issued by a trusted third party, provides independent verification of the existence and state of a digital document at a specific point in time. While both are powerful tools, they serve different primary functions: digital signatures primarily answer the question of "who," while trusted timestamps primarily answer the question of "when." TML's embedding of an ORCID identifier can be seen as a hybrid of these concepts, creating a persistent, self-attesting signature of authorship that is woven into the very fabric of the framework. The legal framework for digital signatures, as exemplified by the laws in the state of California, provides a useful benchmark for evaluating the strength and validity of the attribution mechanism in TML. #### 4.2.2. Trusted Timestamps in Audit Trails: Verifying "When," Not "Who"

Trusted timestamps play a crucial role in the integrity of audit trails, particularly in regulated industries where the chronological sequence of events is paramount. An audit trail is a chronological record that provides documentary evidence of the sequence of activities that have affected a system or a piece of data. In critical systems, such as those used in financial services or pharmaceutical manufacturing, these logs must be tamper-evident and their timestamps must be accurate and reliable . The primary function of a trusted timestamp in this context is to provide irrefutable proof of *when* a particular event occurred. This is essential for reconstructing a sequence of events during an investigation, for proving compliance with regulatory requirements, and for detecting any unauthorized or anomalous activity. For example, regulations like 21 CFR Part 11 in the pharmaceutical industry mandate that audit trails must have secure, computer-generated, and time-synchronized timestamps to ensure their accuracy .

However, a standard trusted timestamp does not, by itself, identify *who* performed the action. While the audit log entry will typically include a user ID or an electronic signature to establish attribution, the timestamp's role is focused on chronology . The naming conventions for audit files themselves often reflect this focus on time. For instance, Oracle's audit file naming convention uses a `start-time.finish-time.machine` format, which is designed to facilitate the retrieval of records from a specific time period . This underscores the primary purpose of timestamps in this context: to create a reliable and searchable chronological backbone for the audit trail. In this sense, a trusted timestamp is a foundational component for ensuring the integrity of the "when," which is a necessary but not sufficient condition for full provenance. The "who" must be established through other means, such as user authentication and digital signatures.
