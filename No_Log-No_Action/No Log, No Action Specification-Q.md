# Cryptographic Locking: A Hardware-Rooted Enforcement Specification for the 'No Log \= No Action' Invariant

## Problem Definition: The Failure Modes of Asynchronous Logging

The foundational premise of this specification is the recognition that conventional logging and telemetry architectures are fundamentally inadequate for ensuring accountability in autonomous systems. These systems, ranging from general-purpose agents to safety-critical cyber-physical platforms like autonomous vehicles and industrial robots, operate under the assumption that their activities can be recorded for later inspection \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. However, this assumption is predicated on a flawed model of data persistence and reliability. Traditional logging is typically designed as an asynchronous background process, decoupled from the primary execution path of the application \[[2](https://blog.csdn.net/liuwill/article/details/142139574)\]. This architectural choice creates a critical temporal gap between the occurrence of an event and its durable recording. An action may be executed, its outcome observed, and then the corresponding log message written; however, if a system failure occurs between the action and the log commit, the action becomes effectively untraceable \[[60](https://kelleycreate.com/what-is-a-vulnerability-assessment/)\]. This lossy nature of logging renders it unsuitable as a reliable mechanism for post-hoc reconstruction or forensic analysis, as the absence of a record cannot be distinguished from a successful but unlogged action \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. Telemetry data, often used interchangeably with logs, suffers from the same vulnerabilities, being susceptible to network packet loss, buffer overflows, or deliberate suppression by malicious components, thus failing to provide a guaranteed audit trail \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\].

This problem is not merely theoretical; it represents a systemic weakness in how modern software is designed. The practice of treating logging as a secondary concern rather than a primary prerequisite for action has led to architectures where accountability is an afterthought. For instance, a crash during a complex operation might leave the system in a partially completed state, with no verifiable record of what was intended versus what was actually accomplished \[[73](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/vihaas-vivek_debugging-embedded-programming-activity-7430172847223775232-yagL)\]. This ambiguity is unacceptable in any system where decisions have significant consequences. The reliance on post-hoc reconstruction is demonstrably insufficient because it operates on incomplete information. If a log entry was never created before an action was taken, there is no evidence to prove the system's intent, making it impossible to verify whether the action was lawful, ethical, or safe according to its governing principles \[[4](https://unstats.un.org/UNSDWebsite/statcom/session_56/documents/BG-3b-Draft_P&R_4th_Rev-E.pdf), [58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. The very definition of an "accountable" system—where every action can be explained and verified—is undermined by the possibility that actions can occur without leaving a trace.

Furthermore, the threat landscape includes adversaries who can actively exploit these weaknesses. An attacker with elevated privileges, such as kernel-level access, can bypass or manipulate the standard logging subsystem entirely, executing arbitrary code without ever generating a log entry \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.03742v1)\]. Even in systems with robust logging, targeted attacks can focus on suppressing specific events deemed sensitive or incriminating. The Robot Operating System (ROS) framework, for example, has been identified as a point of failure due to its initial lack of built-in security features, relying instead on network isolation which is often insufficient against determined attackers \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.23404v1)\]. While subsequent versions like ROS2 have introduced security specifications (DDS Security), their complexity often leads to misconfiguration or non-deployment in practice, creating a dangerous gap between theoretical capability and actual implementation \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.23404v1)\]. A 2025 demonstration highlighted this risk by showing how a compromised build environment could inject trojanized packages that exfiltrate credentials needed to create secure enclave instances, thereby undermining the entire security stack from the ground up \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.23404v1)\]. Such supply chain attacks exemplify how even a system designed with security in mind can be rendered unaccountable if the logging mechanism itself can be compromised at a foundational level. The existence of hardcoded fleet-wide AES keys in some commercial quadruped platforms further illustrates the prevalence of poor security practices that lead to predictable points of failure, where a single compromise can affect an entire class of devices \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.23404v1)\]. These examples collectively demonstrate that the problem of optional logging is not a minor flaw but a critical vulnerability that must be addressed through a hard architectural constraint, transforming logging from a passive record into an active gatekeeper of execution.

## Formalizing the Invariant: Defining 'No Log' and 'Action'

To transform the concept of 'No Log \= No Action' (NLA) from a desirable property into a non-negotiable system law, it must be formally defined with precise, measurable criteria. This specification establishes NLA as a system invariant that governs all executable operations. The formal statement of the invariant is: the system shall permit the initiation or completion of any action only upon the verification of a prior, complete, and locally committed log entry that cryptographically corresponds to that specific action. This redefines the relationship between computation and memory, shifting it from one of recording an event to one where the record is a prerequisite for the event's occurrence. The invariant applies universally across all types of autonomous systems, from embedded controllers to complex AI-driven agents, and encompasses all forms of output, whether logical or physical. The enforcement of this invariant is absolute; no administrative override, software patch, or runtime modification can disable or bypass it. The responsibility for verifying the existence and completeness of the log rests with a dedicated, hardware-rooted execution gate that controls all pathways to action \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1), [46](https://pastel.hal.science/tel-01894734/file/77782_LI_2018_archivage.pdf)\].

A cornerstone of this specification is the rigorous definition of a "log entry." An entry is considered "complete" only when it satisfies three distinct conditions: serialization, cryptographic hashing, and local commitment. First, serialization involves capturing a comprehensive snapshot of the system's context at the moment of decision. This is not a simple text message but a structured, canonical representation of all relevant data, including the action's inputs, the system's internal state, inference results, uncertainty metrics, and a unique timestamp \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. This comprehensive capture ensures that a future auditor has all necessary information to reconstruct the decision-making process. Second, once serialized, the data structure undergoes a cryptographic hash operation using a collision-resistant algorithm such as SHA-256. This step provides an immutable fingerprint of the entire context, ensuring data integrity and enabling efficient comparison \[[1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-031-57931-8.pdf)\]. Third, and most critically, the resulting hash value, along with its metadata, must be durably stored within a protected hardware boundary, such as a secure enclave accumulator or a TPM-backed buffer \[[80](https://docs.trustauthority.intel.com/main/articles/articles/ita/tpm-log.html), [81](https://community.infineon.com/t5/Blogs/Sealing-and-unsealing-data-in-TPM/ba-p/465547)\]. This local commitment guarantees that the log exists in a location inaccessible to the main operating system and resistant to tampering or deletion, fulfilling the requirement that the log precede the action. Only after this three-step process is successfully completed is the log deemed "complete," and only then can the associated action proceed.

Concurrently, the term "action" must be broadly and precisely defined to cover the full spectrum of system outputs. An "action" is any externally visible change of state initiated by the system. This includes, but is not limited to, API calls that modify a database or service state, network packets sent to other systems, commands dispatched to physical actuators in a robotic or vehicular platform, and any state transition within the autonomous agent's own logic. For example, in an autonomous vehicle, an action could be the command to apply the brakes, steer left, or accelerate \[[3](https://www.bmwgroup.com/en/report/2025/downloads/BMW-Group-Report-2025-en.pdf)\]. In a general-purpose agent, an action could be sending an email, posting to a social media feed, or updating a shared knowledge base. The NLA principle applies equally to all these cases. The system's execution path is explicitly gated on the successful commitment of a log corresponding to the *intended* action. This means that intermediate computational steps, such as internal deliberation or inference, may exist within the system's memory but cannot propagate beyond the execution gate until they are accompanied by a valid, committed log entry. This prevents scenarios where the system makes a decision but fails to record it, or records it incorrectly, thereby preserving the fidelity of the system's behavioral history \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. The definition of action is intentionally broad to ensure that no form of consequential output can escape the accounting mechanism.

## Cryptographic Actuator Interlock and Execution Coupling

The 'No Log \= No Action' invariant is enforced through a set of concrete engineering mechanisms that physically and logically couple execution to a cryptographic memory gate. This coupling is realized through a write-before-execute protocol, where the act of committing a log entry is not a separate background task but the primary gatekeeper for all subsequent actions. The system's architecture is inverted from the conventional model (`perform_action(); log_event()`) to a strict pre-commit model (`if (commit_log(action)) { perform_action(); } else { block_execution(); }`). This fundamental shift places the logging subsystem at the center of the execution flow, making it an indispensable prerequisite for any operational output. The enforcement is implemented via a suite of low-level primitives known as execution gating primitives. These primitives are small, highly trusted pieces of code, residing within the hardware root of trust, that halt or block any execution thread attempting to produce an action until a positive acknowledgment of log commitment is received \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1)\]. Should the logging subsystem experience backpressure, such as a full queue, or encounter a failure, such as a hashing error, the gating primitive will stall the pipeline, preventing any unlogged actions from proceeding \[[62](https://www.hs.net/lightdb/docs/html/wal.html)\]. This design directly implements the fail-closed principle, ensuring that the system prioritizes accountability over continued operation in the face of logging uncertainty.

For cyber-physical systems, this abstract concept translates into a tangible cryptographic actuator interlock. Before a command can be sent to a physical component like a motor controller, brake actuator, or steering mechanism, it must be cryptographically bound to a previously committed log. The process works as follows: the system generates a command packet for the actuator. This packet is then signed by a key held exclusively within the secure hardware enclave. Crucially, the signature is generated over a payload that includes not only the command itself but also the cryptographic hash of its corresponding, fully committed log entry \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.03742v1)\]. The physical actuator control unit is programmed with a whitelist of valid signatures and a mechanism to validate the accompanying log hash. Any command arriving without a valid signature or with a log hash that does not correspond to an existing, committed entry in the hardware's memory is silently discarded. This creates a direct, inseparable link between a physical action and its cryptographic proof of origin and intent, making it physically impossible to execute an unauthorized or unlogged actuation. This mechanism extends the principle of Write-Ahead Logging (WAL), commonly used in databases to ensure data consistency, into the domain of real-time actuation and decision-making \[[2](https://blog.csdn.net/liuwill/article/details/142139574), [61](https://www.architecture-weekly.com/p/the-write-ahead-log-a-foundation)\]. In WAL, changes are written to a log before they are applied to the main data store to ensure recovery \[[62](https://www.hs.net/lightdb/docs/html/wal.html)\]. In this TML architecture, the "log" is not just for recovery; it is a real-time authorization token that must be validated *before* the change is permitted to manifest in the external world.

The interaction with transactional systems is a key aspect of this design. The TML logging mechanism is analogous to a high-assurance WAL, but with stronger cryptographic guarantees. While traditional WAL focuses on durability and atomicity for data stores, the TML log focuses on proving the integrity and intent of every action taken by the system \[[61](https://www.architecture-weekly.com/p/the-write-ahead-log-a-foundation)\]. The log serves as the source of truth for all system behaviors, and its cryptographic binding ensures that any attempt to alter the log would invalidate all subsequent actions that depended on it. To manage the volume of logs efficiently, the system employs a Merkle accumulator structure. New log entries are added to the accumulator, which computes a new, compact root hash in near-linear time \[[1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-031-57931-8.pdf)\]. This root hash can be anchored to an external public ledger (as discussed in the dual-lane architecture section) and provides efficient, computationally sound proofs of the inclusion of any individual log entry without needing to expose the entire log history. This allows for scalable auditing and verification. Furthermore, the entire process is designed to be resilient to common system failures. For instance, if a write to a durable storage medium fails, the execution gate will detect the failure and block all actions, triggering a controlled fail-closed state rather than risking an unlogged operation \[[33](https://wiki.unece.org/download/attachments/51971917/TFCS-09-11%20%28Sec%29%20Mitigations%20table%20-%20updated%20during%20TFCS-09.xlsx?api=v2)\]. The use of local, hardware-backed buffers and accumulators ensures that the immediate requirements of the NLA invariant are met even if long-term storage is temporarily unavailable \[[81](https://community.infineon.com/t5/Blogs/Sealing-and-unsealing-data-in-TPM/ba-p/465547)\]. This combination of write-before-execute protocols, cryptographic actuator interlocks, and integration with transactional concepts creates a robust, multi-layered enforcement mechanism that makes bypassing the logging requirement practically impossible.

## Hardware Root of Trust and Adversarial Resistance

The non-bypassable nature of the 'No Log \= No Action' invariant is contingent upon its enforcement within a hardware-rooted trust boundary. Software-only solutions are demonstrably insufficient because a compromised operating system kernel or an authenticated administrator could potentially disable or circumvent the logging mechanism \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.03742v1)\]. To counter this, the core components responsible for logging and execution gating—the serialization logic, the cryptographic hashing engine, the signing keys, and the execution gate itself—must reside and operate exclusively within a hardware-isolated environment. Technologies such as Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone, or dedicated Hardware Security Modules (HSMs) provide the necessary isolation \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1), [46](https://pastel.hal.science/tel-01894734/file/77782_LI_2018_archivage.pdf)\]. These platforms create a secure enclave, a region of memory that is cryptographically isolated from the rest of the system, including the host operating system and privileged firmware \[[14](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3763788), [15](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3696843.3696848)\]. Within this enclave, cryptographic keys required for hashing and signing log entries are generated and stored in a manner that prevents their extraction or observation by any entity outside the hardware boundary \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.03742v1), [45](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.22187)\]. This ensures that the cryptographic foundation of the NLA principle remains uncompromised.

The security of the system is built upon a chain of trust that originates at the silicon level. During the boot process, each stage of the system verifies the integrity of the next before yielding control \[[48](https://www.academia.edu/96645381/A_Multilayer_Secure_Framework_for_Vehicular_Systems)\]. The hardware root of trust measures the firmware, which in turn measures the hypervisor or OS loader, and finally, the OS loads the TML-enclave, whose integrity is measured and attested to by the underlying hardware \[[13](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3664476.3664485), [82](https://ec.europa.eu/research/participants/documents/downloadPublic?documentIds=080166e50b0f754f&appId=PPGMS)\]. This chain of trust culminates in the execution gate, which can only be activated if the entire preceding chain is verified as authentic and untampered. Remote attestation protocols allow an external verifier to obtain a hardware-signed report confirming the exact version and state of the TML enclave, providing strong evidence that the system is running the correct, accountable code \[[42](https://ec.europa.eu/research/participants/documents/downloadPublic?documentIds=080166e5cfd3e9e0&appId=PPGMS), [66](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/2897845.2897895?download=true)\]. This hardware-bound integrity is the ultimate defense against attempts to replace or modify the logging and gating logic. By placing these functions inside a trusted execution environment, the architecture achieves a level of assurance that is impossible to attain in software alone, as the untrusted host environment is prevented from interfering with the enclave's operations \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1)\].

This hardware-centric approach provides inherent resistance against a wide range of adversarial scenarios. Attempts at logging suppression are thwarted because the log's hash is the key that unlocks the next action. Without a valid, committed log, there is no hash to suppress, and the action simply will not be performed. Execution bypass, a common attack vector where a privileged actor tries to skip security checks, is physically prevented by the isolation of the enclave \[[35](https://ec.europa.eu/research/participants/documents/downloadPublic?documentIds=080166e50b5fcb13&appId=PPGMS)\]. Even if an attacker gains full control of the host OS, they cannot directly manipulate the memory or execute code within the secure enclave. The design also resists replay and forgery attacks. Each log entry is cryptographically bound to a unique context, including timestamps and system state, making it computationally infeasible to reuse a log entry for a different action. The use of monotonic counters within the hardware can further enhance security by tracking the total number of operations performed, providing a tamper-resistant metric that can be used for licensing or workload attestation \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.03742v1)\]. For insider threats, the architecture limits the potential for abuse. While an attacker with access to the enclave's private keys could theoretically forge a log, they would still be unable to perform any action without first having the log committed within the hardware boundary, which is a separate and highly secured process. The system's design ensures that accountability is woven into the fabric of its execution, making silent or untraceable actions a logical impossibility.

## System Response: Fail-Closed Behavior and Safe Harbor States

A critical aspect of this specification is the detailed definition of system behavior under failure conditions. The 'No Log \= No Action' invariant mandates a strict fail-closed policy for all possible logging-related errors. The system must be designed to assume the worst-case scenario: that a failure in the logging subsystem indicates an unrecoverable loss of accountability. Consequently, any failure that prevents the successful completion of a log entry must result in the immediate blocking of all subsequent actions. This policy is non-negotiable and applies to a predefined list of failure modes, including, but not limited to: failure of the durable storage medium for log commitment, cryptographic hashing failures, exhaustion of local log buffers leading to queue overflow, and any failure in the hardware root of trust's key management system \[[33](https://wiki.unece.org/download/attachments/51971917/TFCS-09-11%20%28Sec%29%20Mitigations%20table%20-%20updated%20during%20TFCS-09.xlsx?api=v2), [62](https://www.hs.net/lightdb/docs/html/wal.html)\]. When any of these conditions are detected, the execution gate will transition the system into a locked state, refusing to authorize any further operations until the underlying issue is resolved and a clean slate of accountability can be re-established. This fail-closed stance prioritizes verifiability over availability, ensuring that the system never proceeds in an unaccountable state, even at the cost of temporary operational paralysis.

In the context of general-purpose autonomous agents, transitioning to a locked state may be sufficient. However, for cyber-physical systems, a simple halt or "do nothing" state can introduce significant safety risks. For example, abruptly stopping an autonomous vehicle traveling at highway speeds or halting a robotic arm mid-motion could cause damage, injury, or create a hazardous situation. Therefore, this specification requires the definition of a "safe harbor state" for all physical systems. This state is a well-defined, controlled fallback condition that minimizes kinetic energy and maintains system stability while awaiting operator intervention or resolution of the fault \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.23404v1)\]. The transition to a safe harbor state is itself an authorized action, subject to the NLA invariant. The decision to enter this state, along with the reasons for the failure, must be logged and committed before any physical movements begin. Examples of safe harbor states include: for an autonomous vehicle, initiating a controlled deceleration to a stop in the far-right lane, activating hazard lights, and powering down non-essential systems; for a mobile robot, retracting limbs to a neutral pose, braking all motors, and entering a low-power mode. The separation of control stability from decision authority is crucial here; the system must maintain basic stability (e.g., balance) while simultaneously ceding higher-level decision-making authority to a fail-safe protocol \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1)\].

The implementation of these safety protocols requires careful design to prevent paradoxical situations. For instance, the logic that determines when to escalate from a hesitation state to a forced continuation must itself be governed by the NLA principle. Any decision to override the fail-closed state must be preceded by a complete and committed log entry detailing the rationale for the override, the potential risks, and the identity of the entity authorizing it. This prevents unauthorized or impulsive decisions made in moments of crisis. The system must be designed to remain in a state of "hesitation" indefinitely if a safe course of action cannot be securely logged and committed. This design philosophy is aligned with functional safety standards like ISO 26262, which emphasize the importance of fail-safe modes and the mitigation of hazards arising from system malfunctions \[[9](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alaaeddinehamdi_part-iso26262-functionalsafety-activity-7369001824160305152-Qd39), [10](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/en/#!iso:std:68383:en), [38](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/#iso:std:iso:26262:-1:dis:ed-2:v1:en)\]. The ASIL (Automotive Safety Integrity Level) framework, which defines safety requirements based on risk severity, can be used to classify the failure modes and determine the appropriate level of rigor for the logging and safe harbor state mechanisms \[[52](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/349694127_Overview_of_the_Second_Edition_of_ISO_26262_Functional_Safety_-_Road_Vehicles), [56](https://ietresearch.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1049/iet-sen.2013.0236), [71](https://www.scribd.com/document/792929439/Safety-and-Security-of-Cyber-Physical-Systems)\]. For example, a failure that could lead to unintended steering would likely be classified as ASIL D, demanding the highest level of safety mechanisms, including robust logging and fail-closed responses \[[64](https://cp-catapult.s3.amazonaws.com/uploads/2021/07/ATS34-Taxonomy-of-Scenarios-for-Automated-Driving-Report.pdf)\]. By codifying these behaviors, the specification ensures that the pursuit of accountability does not come at the expense of physical safety.

## Dual-Lane Architecture and External Auditability

To address the performance requirements of real-time autonomous systems while maintaining the stringent demands of the NLA invariant, this specification adopts a dual-lane logging architecture. This architecture separates the concerns of local durability from public, decentralized anchoring. The first lane, designated the **Fast Lane**, is optimized for speed and is responsible for establishing local, cryptographic durability within the hardware root of trust. Every action must trigger a Fast Lane commitment, which involves serializing the action context, computing its cryptographic hash, and committing it to a durable, tamper-evident store accessible only by the secure enclave. The Fast Lane is engineered to meet a strict sub-2ms latency requirement for this commitment process, ensuring that it does not become a bottleneck for time-sensitive operations \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1)\]. This rapid local commitment is what satisfies the immediate NLA invariant, providing the cryptographic guarantee that a verifiable record of the action exists *before* it is released.

The second lane, the **Slow Lane**, handles the asynchronous process of anchoring the commitments made in the Fast Lane to a public, append-only ledger or blockchain. The purpose of this lane is to provide long-term, external, and real-time verifiability of the system's log \[[58](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395305525_Trust_but_Verify_Audit-ready_logging_for_clinical_AI)\]. Anchoring a hash to a public ledger creates a decentralized, trust-minimized proof that the log existed at a certain point in time and has not been altered \[[1](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-031-57931-8.pdf)\]. Because this process often involves network communication and consensus mechanisms, it can take significantly longer than the Fast Lane commitment—potentially seconds or minutes—and is therefore not suitable for gating immediate execution. The critical insight is that the delay in Slow Lane anchoring does not violate the NLA principle. The invariant only requires the log to be committed *before* the action; it does not require the log to be publicly proven before the action. Local durability achieved in the Fast Lane is sufficient to satisfy the immediate accountability requirement. The Slow Lane adds a valuable layer of public auditability but is a separate, non-blocking process. Backpressure or circuit breaker mechanisms must be implemented in the Slow Lane to manage network congestion or ledger saturation, but these should not affect the operation of the Fast Lane or the ability to commit logs locally \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.16448v1)\].

This dual-lane model provides a balanced solution for achieving both high performance and strong accountability. Real-time systems can operate with minimal latency, as their primary logging requirement (local commitment) is extremely fast. At the same time, the system produces a continuously growing, cryptographically chained record of its behavior that can be independently verified by any external auditor. This addresses the requirement for both internal verifiability (ensured by the hardware-rooted Fast Lane) and external real-time auditability (enabled by the Slow Lane). The system's architecture must support this duality by maintaining two distinct queues: one for local Fast Lane processing and another for batched submission to the Slow Lane. The separation ensures that a failure or slowdown in public anchoring has no impact on the system's ability to function accountably in the short term. This design is particularly relevant for applications requiring compliance with evolving regulatory frameworks, such as those concerning digital assets or autonomous vehicle data, where a persistent, verifiable, and auditable record is becoming a legal necessity. The ability to generate a Proof of Timely-Retrievability (PoTR) can further strengthen the auditability claim, allowing an auditor to verify not only that a log entry was committed but also that it was retrievable within a specified timeframe \[[19](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3685935)\].
